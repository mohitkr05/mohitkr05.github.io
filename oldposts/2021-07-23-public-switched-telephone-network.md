The PSTN is known as the Public Switched Telephone Network or more commonly the Wired Telephone Network. The PSTN is generally governed by the ITU-T technical standard [E.163 / E.164][1] (also called phone number). The PSTN is a _circuit switched_ public network originally intended for telephone systems. The PSTN is the world's first and largest telecommunication network. 

The main digital circuitry of the PSTN is a 64 kbit/s channel called "DS0" or digital signal 0. DS0 is also called the timeslots because it is multiplexed together in a time-division manner. In a typical end-to-end call, speech is digitally converted using 8-bit pulse code modulation at a sample rate of 8 kHz. 

In a higher capacity scenario multiple circuits can be multiplexed together, for example, 24 DS0s can be connected to form a DS1 or T1 signal (European version of E1 has 32 x 64 kbps channels). 

The PSTN has been the only telecommunications network available over the phone for over hundred years. Cellular radio access networks that broadcast over PSTN _trunk networks_ are becoming more and more popular today. Other network operators include Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN), Digital Shared Line (DSL), Asynchronous Transport Mode (ATM), Frame Relay, and VoIP over the Internet. 

**History**

PSTN was the first traffic engineering example that provides quality of service (QoS) guarantees. _Erlang (1878-1929)_ established the mathematical foundation of the methods needed to determine the number and configuration of equipment and the number of personnel required to provide a specific level of service. 

In the 1970s, the telecommunications industry began to use the X.25 protocol to implement packet-switched network services, which was carried on most end-to-end devices already in use on the PSTN. In the 1980s, the industry began planning digital services, assuming that they would follow the same model as voice services, and conceived the vision of end-to-end circuit-switched services, the so-called band integrated service digital network. -ISDN). 

The vision of B-ISDN has been replaced by the disruptive technology of the Internet. Only the oldest part of the telephone network still uses analog technology to handle anything beyond the end user’s last mile loop. In recent years, more and more end users are using digital services. 

Many observers believe that the long-term future of PSTN will be just an Internet application; however, the Internet still has a long way to go before this transformation can be achieved. QoS guarantee is an aspect of VoIP technology that needs improvement. There are many large private telephone networks that are not connected to the PSTN, usually for military purposes. There are also private networks operated by large companies that connect to the PSTN through limited gateways, such as large private exchanges (PBX). 

The first phones did not have a network, but they were privately used and connected in pairs. Users who want to talk to different people have as many phones as they need for this purpose. The user who wants to speak will whistle to the transmitter until the other party hears it. However, soon, a bell for _signalling_ and _switch-hook_ was added, and the telephone took advantage of the exchange principle already adopted in the telegraph network. 

Each telephone is connected to a local telephone exchange, and the exchange is connected to a trunk line. These networks are interconnected in a hierarchical manner, covering cities, countries, continents and oceans.
